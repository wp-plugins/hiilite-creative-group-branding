The Beauty of Money

Voices from the Engraver, a brand new large and colourful traveling exhibit created by the Bank of Canada Museum in Ottawa in partnership with the Canadian Museum of History will make its first appearance in BC at the Okanagan Heritage Museum from June to October.

Do you ever post a letter or buy your lunch with art? You do it every day with stamps and money! From concept to final product, this interactive display allows you to experience the creative process, the technical skill and sheer artistry that underlie Canadian stamps and bank notes. See famous historic examples with local Okanagan images, such as the $100 bill with a picture of Okanagan Lake on the back, or the recent new stamps with “Pansies,” painted by Laurie Koss of Kelowna. Laurie will be on hand at our opening event to share her work with us, we encourage all our volunteers, members, and the public to attend the event and meet the artist! As part of the opening we will be serving refreshments and providing various activities for attendees, including the opportunity to create your own stamp or replica money. Please join us for the opening on Saturday June 6th at 3pm at the Okanagan Heritage Museum.

Any stamp or coin collectors in your life? Watch for the special programs and workshops planned to accompany this fascinating exhibit.

Asian Heritage Month on Display

May is Asian Heritage Month, and the Okanagan Heritage Museums is showcasing Asian artefacts and the local Asian Canadian cultures with a colourful new exhibit. Created in collaboration with the Asian Heritage Okanagan Committee, the display dances through the Exhibition Gallery with lots of information, personal stories and artefacts shared by the several Asian Canadian communities of Kelowna. In addition, a special display room showcases precious and rarely seen Asian objects from the Museum’s artefact collection. Don’t wait to come see this colourful display. You will want to return for another look with friends before it disappears at the end of May.

Ogopogo, Don’t Cha Know?

KMS helped facilitate a special UBCO student project this spring. Lauren Richardson and Samantha Steenwyk created a display about N’ha-a-itk and the Ogopogo.

“For our project, we decided to take a look at how the Ogopogo is related to the sacred spirit of the lake – N’ha-a-itk. For the Syilx people, N’ha-a-itk represents a sacred connection to the land.”

The students hope to make visitors aware of the significance of N’ha-a-itk and the role settlers played in transforming the entity into the Ogopogo.

New WWI Exhibition
Explore The Glory Passes at the Okanagan Military Museum

When WWI began, there were predictions it would be over before Christmas. However, winter came and went and the fighting continued with no end in sight. Troops realized that battle conditions were much worse than they expected. They faced brutal physical conditions and unforeseen psychological stresses. Some soldiers used humour to cope with these challenges. For many, the glory of battle passed quickly.
